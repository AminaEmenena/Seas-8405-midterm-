📊 1. Risk Management & Business Impact Analysis
Key Definition: The risk management process is a formal method of evaluating vulnerabilities. What makes it formal is FISMA — it must be documented and in writing. A robust process identifies vulnerabilities, assesses impact and likelihood, and generates a formal assessment of possible mitigations.
Key Term — Know This Risk Assessment = Risk Identification (they are the same thing)

Business Impact Analysis (BIA)

A BIA is a functional analysis that occurs as part of business continuity and planning for disaster recovery. It helps the organization understand what impact a disruptive event would have. The resulting document lists critical business functions, their resource dependencies, and their level of criticality.

Exam Format: "Which of the following is NOT one of the 4 BIA steps?"
StepActionWhat You Do
1Identify critical processes and resourcesBCP committee identifies all business units. Gather data via questionnaires, interviews, surveys. Document all business assets including physical, financial, and competitive advantage.
2Identify outage impacts and estimate downtimeAnalyze the impact of losing each resource on ability to do business. Provides raw material for redundancy metrics.
3Identify resource requirementsDetermine all resources needed for each process — includes device name, OS, hardware specs, and HUMAN resources (people are just as critical as technology).
4Identify recovery prioritiesEstablish priorities based on process criticality, outage impacts, tolerable downtime, and system resources. Three levels: High, Medium, Low.
Important Distinction: The BIA stipulates recovery priorities but does NOT provide recovery solutions. Those solutions are given in the Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP). The BIA relies heavily on any vulnerability analysis and risk assessment performed by the BCP committee or a separately appointed risk assessment team.

Risk Assessment — 4 Main Goals

  1. Identify assets and asset value — start with an asset inventory
  2. Identify vulnerabilities and threats — what could go wrong?
  3. Calculate threat probability and business impact — how likely and how bad?
  4. Balance threat impact with countermeasure cost — don't deploy a $10,000 hammer if a $5 one works

Risk assessment should be carried out before any mergers, acquisitions, or new technology deployments. If not supported and directed by senior management, it will not be successful.

🎯 2. Identifying Critical Systems

When identifying critical processes and resources, the BCP committee must first identify all business units or functional areas. After identification, the team selects individuals responsible for gathering needed data using techniques like questionnaires, interviews, and surveys.

During data gathering, document all business processes, functions, resources they depend on, and all business assets — including physical assets, financial assets, and any assets providing competitive advantage or credibility. Then determine the criticality level by analyzing the impact that losing each resource would have on the capability to continue doing business.

Professor Duke: "Who determines what's critical? The C-suite — not the CISO. Your job as a cyber champion is to support and provide good cyber means as a business enabler to critical systems. Don't stick your neck out where it doesn't belong. I've seen a couple of cyber champions put their nose where it didn't belong and they got their head cut off."
Don't play favorites: Professor Duke told a story about a cyber chief who prioritized his golf buddy's logistics systems over HR's critical systems. "Put your money where your mouth is — fix critical systems first and don't play favoritism."

⚖️ 4. Qualitative vs Quantitative Risk Analysis

To make a risk determination, an organization must perform a formal risk analysis asking: What corporate critical assets need protection? What are the business needs? What outside threats are most likely?

TechniquesIntuition, experience, brainstorming, focus groups, surveys, questionnaires, Delphi techniqueEquations — SLE, ALE, ARO calculations

Best ForIntangible assetsTangible assets

Delphi Technique: A method used to estimate the likelihood and outcome of future events. Each group member independently uses their experience to rank the likelihood of each threat and the damage that might result. After ranking, data is combined into a report for management. Requires a risk assessment team with experience and education related to assessing threats.
Professor Duke: "I used a hybrid approach my whole career and it kept everybody happy. I gave Colin Powell the red, yellow, and green (qualitative). I gave the bean counters the numbers (quantitative). Most risk analysis includes some hybrid of both. Keep it simple — if you make quantitative too complex, it will backfire."
Important: Even though quantitative uses numeric values, a purely quantitative analysis cannot be achieved because some level of subjectivity is always part of the data. Estimates should be based on historical data, industry experience, and expert opinion.
🔢 5. Risk Equations — SLE & ALE
Key Formulas SLE = AV × EF    |    ALE = SLE × ARO
VariableFull NameDefinition
AVAsset ValueThe monetary value of the asset
EFExposure FactorThe percentage of value or functionality that will be lost when a threat event occurs
SLESingle Loss ExpectancyThe monetary impact of each threat occurrence
AROAnnualized Rate of OccurrenceThe estimate of how often a given threat might occur annually
ALEAnnual Loss ExpectancyThe expected risk factor of an annual threat event

Worked Example

An organization has a web server farm with an Asset Value (AV) of $20,000. The risk assessment determined that a power failure is a threat agent. The Exposure Factor (EF) for a power failure is 25%.

SLE = $20,000 × 0.25 = $5,000

If the ARO for the power failure is 50% (occurs once every two years):

ALE = $5,000 × 0.50 = $2,500

Professor Duke's Warning: "These equations are for you and your risk management team. They're NOT for exposure to the C-suite. The minute you brief these to the C-suite, they're going to chew you up. Good bean counters like to get their hands on equations and they'll twist them — before you know it, you went in thinking you were going to gain something and you're 10 steps behind. Keep these internal."
🗣️ 6. Communication of Risk & Risk Handling

Technical cybersecurity risk is largely misunderstood by non-technical people. As a security professional, you must bridge the knowledge gap by translating risks into business terms the audience understands.

MOU vs SLA

MOU (Memorandum of Understanding): NOT legally binding. Indicates a general agreement between parties to work together. May contain security requirements.

SLA (Service Level Agreement): Specifies a service to be provided by a certain party. May include specifications that inhibit or prevent deployment of certain measures. Whoever frames the most risk usually starts the SLA.

Compensating Controls

Compensating controls are put in place to substitute for a primary access control and mainly act to mitigate risks. By using them, you can reduce risk to a more manageable level.

  • Examples: Requiring two authorized signatures to release sensitive info. Requiring two keys owned by different personnel to open a safety deposit box.
  • Requirement: Must be recorded along with the reason the primary control was not implemented. This is part of the formal risk management process.

Tabletop Exercises

Definition: A tabletop exercise is an informal brainstorming session — the most cost-effective and efficient way of identifying areas of vulnerability before moving on to higher-level testing. Participants discuss scenarios to determine if there are gaps in their response plans.

🔗 8. Supply Chain Assessment & Hardware Authenticity

Organizational risk mitigation requires assessing the safety and integrity of hardware and software in the supply chain. Stories about counterfeit gear containing backdoors have circulated for years — and they're real.

Vendor Due Diligence

When performing due diligence with a vendor, you are assessing their products and services for innate security. While functionality and value matter, security professionals are even more concerned about whether products contain hidden vulnerabilities or backdoors.

OEM Documentation

Definition: One way to reduce likelihood of counterfeit equipment is to insist on verifiable Original Equipment Manufacturing (OEM) documentation. This paperwork often includes anti-counterfeiting features. Use the vendor's website to verify all identifying numbers.

Common Criteria (NIAP)

The Common Criteria is a website maintained by NSA's department called NIAP (National Information Assurance Partnership). It provides:

  • EALs (Evaluation Assurance Levels): Confidence rating that a product has been properly tested and vetted
  • Protection Profiles: Sets of security requirements for a category of products
Professor Duke: "At a conference in Nashville, I saw vendors with signs saying 'Pentagon Approved' and 'NSA Approved.' The Pentagon doesn't approve technology — that's not their business. And NSA doesn't approve things either — the Common Criteria does through NIAP. Perform your due care and due diligence. Don't just buy it and check a box."
Real-World Warning: The Pentagon renovation had vendors who bought non-OEM devices with backdoors. Millions wasted, people fired — both civilian and military. DISA posted a warning about Chinese-manufactured computers arriving pre-infected with firmware-level viruses: "5 out of 20 came infected."
