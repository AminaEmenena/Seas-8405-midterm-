TL;DR — Key Concepts
  • Risk Management is a formal process (FISMA requires documentation)
  • BIA: 4 steps — identify critical processes, outage impacts, resource requirements, recovery priorities
  • Qualitative = subjective (Delphi, brainstorming); Quantitative = monetary values (SLE, ALE)
  • SLE = AV x EF; ALE = SLE x ARO
  • Risk Handling: Avoidance, Transfer, Mitigation, Acceptance
  • Red Team = attackers; Blue Team = defenders; White Team = referees
  • Tabletop Exercise: most cost-effective way to identify vulnerabilities
  • Compensating Controls: must be documented with reason primary control was not implemented
  • C-suite determines what systems are critical, NOT the CISO
  • Communicate risk in business terms to executives — never talk technical to non-technical people
1. Risk Management & BIA
Risk management = FORMAL method of evaluating vulnerabilities. FISMA makes it formal — must be in writing.
Exam tip: "Which is NOT one of the 4 BIA steps?"

4 BIA Steps:

  1. Identify critical processes and resources
  2. Identify outage impacts and estimate downtime
  3. Identify resource requirements
  4. Identify recovery priorities

BIA relies on vulnerability analysis and risk assessment. Performed by BCP committee or separate team.

Key TermRisk Assessment = Risk Identification (same thing)
2. Identifying Critical Systems
  • BCP committee identifies all business units/functional areas
  • Document processes, functions, resources, dependencies
  • Include physical, financial, and competitive advantage assets
  • Resource requirements include HUMAN resources
  • Recovery priorities: High, Medium, Low
  • BIA stipulates priorities but NOT solutions (those are in DRP)
Professor Duke: "C-suite decides what's critical, NOT the CISO. Cyber is a business enabler. Don't stick your neck out where it doesn't belong."
3. Recoverability & Fault Tolerance
  • Recoverability: Ability to recover from disaster. Minimize downtime. Emphasis on personnel + resources.
  • Fault Tolerance: Backup begins when primary fails. Key: lack of service interruption. Backup may not match primary service level. Varying levels based on budget.

Delphi Technique: Method to estimate likelihood and outcome of future events. Each member independently ranks threats, data combined into report.
Professor Duke: "I used a hybrid approach my whole career. Give Colin Powell the red/yellow/green and give the bean counters the numbers."
5. Risk Equations — SLE & ALE
FormulasSLE = AV × EF   |   ALE = SLE × ARO

Example: Web server farm AV = $20,000. Power failure EF = 25%, ARO = 50%.

SLE = $20,000 × 0.25 = $5,000. ALE = $5,000 × 0.50 = $2,500.

Warning: These equations are for YOUR risk team. Don't show them to the C-suite — financial people will twist them and take you down a rabbit hole.
6. Communication of Risk & Risk Handling

Audience matters: Translate technical risks to BUSINESS terms. Focus on disruption, regulatory issues, bad press. Never talk technical to non-technical people.

4 Methods to Handle Risk

MethodDescription
AvoidanceTerminate risky activity or choose less risky alternative
TransferPass to third party (insurance)
MitigationReduce risk to acceptable level
AcceptanceUnderstand and accept risk and cost

Compensating controls: Substitute for primary controls. Must be DOCUMENTED with reason primary wasn't implemented. Examples: two signatures, two keys.

7. Red / Blue / White Teams (MATCHING)

Tabletop Exercise: Most cost-effective and efficient. Informal brainstorming session.

Risk FormulaRisk = Threat × Vulnerability × Impact / Countermeasures
8. Supply Chain & Hardware Authenticity
  • Vendor due diligence: Assess vendor products/services for innate security
  • OEM documentation: Anti-counterfeiting features, verifiable ID numbers. Verify via vendor website.
  • Common Criteria (NIAP): NSA-managed. EALs (Evaluation Assurance Levels) and Protection Profiles for hardware/software authenticity.
  • Counterfeit gear contains backdoors — Pentagon had incidents with vendors buying non-OEM equipment.
