TL;DR — Key Concepts
  • 6 Steps of IR: Detect, Respond, Report, Recover, Remediate, Review
  • 3 R's: Redundancy, Remediation, Resiliency
  • > 24 hours = disaster (not just an incident)
  • MTD/MPTD: max tolerable downtime; RTO must be smaller than MTD
  • WRT = MTD minus RTO
  • MTBF: calculated by the device vendor, not the org
  • Criticality: Critical (min/hrs), Urgent (24h), Important (72h), Normal (7d), Nonessential (30d)
  • Comms: out-of-band with E2E encryption (not corporate email/VoIP)
  • Senior leadership support is the most important factor for IR success
  • Lessons learned: first doc after recovery; changes go through change control
1. Communication Plan & Secure Comms
Key Principle: All IR communications must use out-of-band methods with end-to-end encryption (e.g., OTR Messaging). Do NOT use corporate email or VoIP — they may be compromised.
  • Limit communication to designated trusted parties only
  • Disclose based on regulatory/legislative requirements (HIPAA Breach Notification Rule, state laws)
  • Prevent inadvertent release — all public/press info handled by PR (one person, consistent message)
  • Timing of all communications should be specified in the plan
Professor Duke: "When I see two or three people start to speak for the company, that's a red flag — disjointed capabilities. Remember Al Haig — he stood up and said 'I'm in charge' when the VP should have spoken. He was fired that day."

6. Containment & Eradication

Segmentation

  • Layer 3: Barriers based on IP subnets (physical LANs or VLANs), using ACLs on routers
  • Layer 2: VLAN, private VLAN, port security
  • Process isolation: Prevents buffer overflow by dedicating memory per process
  • DMZ: For most vulnerable devices (Wi-Fi devices at home)

Secure Disposal (Slide 61)

MethodResult
ClearingNormal recovery impossible, but forensic recovery possible
Purging (Sanitization)Unrecoverable even with advanced forensics
DestructionPhysical destruction or degaussing (magnetic randomization)
Caution: SSDs/flash drives cannot always be overwritten. Research vendor sanitation commands. NSA and the Navy both got embarrassed by improper disposal — classified data found on auctioned systems.
7. Post-Incident Activities
  • Lessons Learned Report (Slide 71): FIRST document drafted. What went right/wrong? How improve? What change? What was cost?
  • Change Control Process (Slide 72): All changes go through change control board. FORMAL process — examined for unforeseen consequences.
  • Incident Summary Report (Slide 74): Each stakeholder gets their own version. Includes: when detected, scope, containment, recovery work, effective areas, improvements needed.
  • Evidence Retention: Store securely until presented in court. Protect chain of custody. (San Bernardino: cloned iPhones, called Israel, preserved original for court.)
The 3 R's Redundancy • Remediation • Resiliency
Key Distinction: Incident < 24 hours. Disaster > 24 hours. Review IR plan every 12 months.
8. Reverse Engineering

Three ways binary malware can be made readable:

MethodDescription
DisassemblyReading machine code into memory, outputting each instruction as text
DecompilingReconstructing high-level language source code
DebuggingStepping through code interactively
  • Kernel debugger: Operates at ring 0 — direct kernel access. Must inspect from OUTSIDE the operating system.
  • Usermode debugger: Access only to usermode space. Sufficient most of the time, but not for rootkits or advanced threats.
