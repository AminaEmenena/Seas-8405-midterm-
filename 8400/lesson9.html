🔐 1. CIA Triad — Foundation of Security

The three fundamentals of security are confidentiality, integrity, and availability (CIA). Most security issues result in a violation of at least one facet of the CIA triad. Understanding these principles helps ensure that security controls protect at least one of them.

Key Exam Fact The OPPOSITE of confidentiality = DISCLOSURE
ComponentDefinitionOppositeControls
ConfidentialityPrevent disclosure of data to unauthorized entities. Sensitivity level must be determined before access controls are put in place.DisclosureEncryption, steganography, ACLs, data classification
IntegrityCorrectness, completeness, and soundness of data. Data cannot be modified except by authorized parties.AlterationHashing, digital signatures, version control
AvailabilityResources must be accessible when needed by authorized users.Destruction/DisruptionRedundancy, backups, disaster recovery
Don't Forget: Data can also exist in printed format. Appropriate controls should protect data on the network, but printed data also needs protection through data disposal policies. Higher sensitivity = more access controls.

🌍 3. GDPR & Data Sovereignty

The EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) aims primarily to give individuals control over their personal data and to simplify the regulatory environment for international business by unifying regulation within the EU.

Professor Duke: "I suspect that this will be a matching question."

GDPR — 6 Lawful Purposes for Processing Personal Data (Article 6)

Personal data may not be processed unless there is at least one legal basis to do so:

  1. Consent: The data subject has given consent to processing of their personal data
  2. Contractual obligations: To fulfill contractual obligations with a data subject, or for tasks at the request of a data subject entering a contract
  3. Legal obligations: To comply with a data controller's legal obligations
  4. Vital interests: To protect the vital interests of a data subject or another individual
  5. Public interest: To perform a task in the public interest or in official authority
  6. Legitimate interests: For the legitimate interests of a data controller or third party, unless overridden by interests/rights of the data subject (especially children)
EU Electronic Security Directive: Defines electronic signature principles. A signature must be uniquely linked to the signer and to the data, so any subsequent data change is detectable.

Data Sovereignty (Slides 23-24)

Definition: Data sovereignty is the concept that data stored in digital format is subject to the laws of the country in which the data is located. This is further complicated by cloud solutions, where data may reside in multiple countries.
Exam Format: "Which one of these is NOT one of the four questions?" Security professionals should answer:
1. Where is the data stored?
2. Who has access to the data?
3. Where is the data backed up?
4. How is the data encrypted?
🛡️ 4. Data Privacy Principles

Data Minimization (Slide 25)

Organizations should minimize the amount of personal data they store to what is necessary. This is an important GDPR principle. By reducing personal data, the attack surface is also reduced. Data processing should only use as much data as required to accomplish a given task.

Purpose Limitation (Slide 26)

Personal data collected for one purpose cannot be repurposed without further consent from the individual. This is another key GDPR principle finding wide adoption globally.

Example: Data collected to track a disease outbreak cannot be used to identify individuals. The purpose was disease tracking, not individual identification.

Employee Privacy (Slide 21)

Employee privacy issues must be addressed to protect organizations from costly legal penalties. Organizations must give employees proper notice of any monitoring and apply monitoring consistently.

No-Expectation-of-Privacy Policy: Many organizations implement this policy, which the employee must sign after receiving appropriate training. It should specifically describe unacceptable behavior. Keep in mind that some actions are still protected by the Fourth Amendment. Consult legal counsel when designing monitoring solutions.

Non-Disclosure Agreements (Slide 27)

An NDA must be signed by any entity that has access to information that is part of a trade secret. Anyone who signs an NDA will suffer legal consequences if the organization proves they violated it. NDAs are a critical tool for protecting intellectual property including patents, copyrights, and trade secrets.

📁 5. Data Retention

A data retention policy outlines how various data types must be retained and may rely on the data classifications described in the organization's data classification policy. Retention requirements vary based on data type, data age, and legal/regulatory requirements.

What a Data Retention Policy Covers

  • Purpose of the policy and portions of the org affected
  • Any exclusions to the policy
  • Personnel responsible for overseeing the policy and for data destruction
  • Data types covered: HR data, accounts payable/receivable, sales data, customer data, email
  • Retention schedule for each data type

Implementation (Slide 36)

The organization must consider the media and hardware used to retain data. Once drafted, the policy must be formally adopted. Personnel must be trained to comply. Auditing and monitoring should be configured to ensure compliance. Data owners should review periodically to determine if changes are needed.

Key Principle: For most organizations, a one-size-fits-all solution is impossible because of different data types. Only those most familiar with each data type (data owner or stakeholder) can determine the best retention policy. The security professional should be involved in design but acts in an ADVISORY ROLE only — providing expertise, not decisions.
🔒 6. Technical Controls — DLP, DRM & Data Masking

Data Loss Prevention — DLP (Slide 30)

Definition: DLP systems are used to prevent data exfiltration, which is the intentional or unintentional loss of sensitive data from the network. DLP monitors data in motion, at rest, and in use.
Professor Duke: "2027 will be the year cryptography and computing moves to quantum."

Data Masking (Slide 31)

Data masking means altering data from its original state to protect it. There are several techniques:

TechniqueDescriptionExample
EncryptionStoring data in encrypted formAES-256 encrypted database
HashingStoring a hash value instead of original dataPassword stored as SHA-256 hash
SubstitutionReplacing data with realistic but fake valuesReplacing real SSN with fake one
TranspositionRearranging data elementsShuffling character positions

Digital Rights Management — DRM (Slides 35-39)

Hardware manufacturers, publishers, and copyright holders use DRM to control the use of digital content. The U.S. Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) of 1998 imposes criminal penalties on those who make available technologies to circumvent content protection.
