📋 1. Policies, Procedures & Controls

Organizations use policies, procedures, and controls to implement security. Creating and implementing them can be a challenge, but security frameworks provide templates, examples, and guidance to help.

TermDefinitionExample
PolicyBroad statement that defines the aim of the security measure"All data must be encrypted at rest"
ProcedureDefines how to carry out the measuresStep-by-step guide to enable BitLocker encryption
ControlCountermeasure or mitigation used to prevent breachesAES-256 encryption, firewalls, access controls
FrameworkA structured set of guidelines that helps organizations develop security programsNIST CSF, COBIT, ISO 27000
Key Point: Policies are living documents. Don't create a policy, put it on the shelf, and come back three years later. In three years it might be old, dead, and not relevant — especially given today's AI environment.
🏛️ 2. Risk-Based Frameworks — COBIT & TOGAF

COBIT 2019 — 5 Domains

The governance and management objectives in COBIT 2019 are grouped into five domains. The domains have names with verbs that express the key purpose and areas of activity.

#DomainAcronymPurpose
1Evaluate, Direct, and MonitorEDMGovernance — setting direction and monitoring
2Align, Plan, and OrganizeAPOStrategic alignment of IT with business
3Build, Acquire, and ImplementBAISolutions development and deployment
4Deliver, Service, and SupportDSSOperations and service delivery
5Monitor, Evaluate, and AssessMEAPerformance monitoring and compliance

The COBIT 2019 Goals Cascade supports translation of enterprise goals into priorities for alignment goals, flowing from Stakeholder Drivers → Enterprise Goals → Alignment Goals → Governance and Management Objectives.

TOGAF — The Open Group Architecture Framework

TOGAF helps organizations design, plan, implement, and govern enterprise information architecture. It is based on four architecture types:

ArchitectureFocus
BusinessStrategy, governance, organization, and key business processes
ApplicationIndividual systems, interactions, and relationships to core processes
DataStructure of logical and physical data assets
TechnologyHardware, software, and network infrastructure
ADM (Architecture Development Method): TOGAF's prescribed approach for developing enterprise architecture. The process is iterative and cyclic — each step checks with requirements. Flows through: Architecture Vision → Business Architecture → Information Systems → Technology Architecture → Opportunities → Migration Planning → Implementation Governance → Architecture Change Management.
🛡️ 3. NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0

NIST's Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 was released in February 2024. It now explicitly aims to help ALL organizations — not just those in critical infrastructure (its original target). It is designed for all audiences, industry sectors, and organization types, regardless of cybersecurity sophistication.

The Big Change in CSF 2.0 GOVERN is the NEW 6th function!
FunctionPurposeKey Categories
IdentifyHelp determine current cybersecurity risk to the organizationAsset Management, Risk Assessment, Improvement
ProtectUse safeguards to prevent or reduce cybersecurity riskIdentity Management & Access Control, Awareness & Training, Data Security, Platform Security
DetectFind and analyze possible cybersecurity attacks and compromisesContinuous Monitoring, Adverse Event Analysis
RespondTake action regarding a detected cybersecurity incidentIncident Management, Analysis, Reporting & Communication, Mitigation
RecoverRestore assets and operations impacted by a cybersecurity incidentRecovery Plan Execution, Recovery Communication
Govern (NEW!)Establish and monitor the organization's cybersecurity risk management strategy, expectations, and policyOrganizational Context, Risk Management Strategy, Supply Chain Risk Mgmt, Roles & Responsibilities, Policies & Procedures, Oversight
Supply Chain Risk Management was originally under the Identify function. In CSF 2.0, it was moved to Govern, adding key outcomes: ensuring suppliers are known and prioritized by criticality, performing due diligence before entering supply chain relationships, and integrating supply chain security into the organization's cybersecurity program.

Profiles

Profiles help organizations understand how closely their current cybersecurity objectives map to the framework's functions, and lay out their desired state to identify gaps and create action plans. CSF 2.0 significantly expanded guidance on profiles with examples and detailed steps for creating and using them.

CSF 2.0 Profile Process Define use case & scope Profile │ Gather needed information │ Create Current and Target Profiles │ Analyze Profiles & create action plan │ Implement action plan & update Profiles │ Repeat...
🌐 4. ISO 27000, SABSA, ITIL & CMMI
FrameworkFull NamePurposeKey Detail
ISO/IEC 27000International Organization for Standardization + International Electrotechnical CommissionSecurity program development standard for developing and maintaining an ISMSStandardized British Standard 7799 (BS7799). ISO 27001 = general standard (on CISSP exam)
SABSASherwood Applied Business Security ArchitectureEnterprise security architecture framework6 communication questions (What, Where, When, Why, Who, How) × 6 layers (operational, component, physical, logical, conceptual, contextual). Risk-driven. Open-use methodology, not commercial.
ITILInformation Technology Infrastructure LibraryProcess management standard (OMB Circular A-130, owned by AXELOS since 2013)Primarily concerned with managing SLAs. 5 core publications, 26 processes. Independent review of security controls every 3 years.

Don't Confuse: CMMI (software development maturity) vs CMMC (defense contractor cybersecurity certification). They sound similar but are completely different frameworks with different purposes.

Certification vs Accreditation

Certification evaluates the technical system components — plug in the cable, test the security elements.

Accreditation evaluates the adequacy of the system's overall security — the big picture assessment.

Order matters: Certify first, then accredit.

Maturity Models

Maturity models are used to determine where you are in the continual improvement process as it relates to security. They offer help in reaching a higher level of improvement. Think of them as a roadmap from ad hoc security practices to optimized, measured, and continuously improving ones.

🔑 5. Password Types (Slides 27-29 — BIG MULTIPLE CHOICE)
Professor Duke: "Slides 27, 28, 29 will be big multiple choice on the final exam."

Password authentication is the most popular method implemented today. Before looking at password policies, you must understand all the types of passwords that can be used:

TypeDescriptionAdvantageDisadvantage
Standard WordSingle words with mixture of upper/lowercaseEasy to rememberEasy for attackers to crack
Combination (Composition)Mix of two unrelated dictionary words. Can include upper/lowercase and numbers.Harder to break than standardCan be hard to remember
StaticSame password for each login. Never changes.SimpleMinimum security. Most often seen in peer-to-peer networks.
ComplexMixture of upper/lowercase, numbers, AND special characters. Often enforced by organization policy.Very hard to crackHarder to remember, hard to enter correctly
PassphraseLong phrase used as password. Length is the key advantage.Easier to remember, much harder to attackMay be slow to type
CognitiveInformation to verify identity — user answers life questions (favorite color, pet's name, mother's maiden name)Users usually easily rememberSomeone with intimate knowledge (spouse, child, sibling) may know the answers
One-Time (OTP / Dynamic)Used only once to log in, then discardedHIGHEST LEVEL OF SECURITY — discarded after single use
Graphical (CAPTCHA)Graphics as part of authentication. "Completely Automated Public Turing test to tell Computers and Humans Apart"Ensures a human, not a machine is authenticatingCan be inconvenient for users
NumericOnly numbers. Limited by digit count.Simple to implement4 digits = only 10,000 possibilities (0000-9999). Easy to crack once attacker knows it's numeric-only.

Password Management

  • Password life: NIST current standard = 90 days (previously 60)
  • Password history: How long before a password can be reused
  • Linux passwords: Stored in a special file known as the shadow file. Because the password is a text file that can be easily accessed, ensure Linux servers use the shadow file and protect passwords with a hash.
  • Account lockout policies: Implemented at the enterprise level on servers that manage the network
Professor Duke's Advice: Review password policies with your cyber team once every two or three months. System administrators are in a burnout job — password policies get abused when they're not monitored properly.
🎛️ 6. Control Categories & Types

Control categories refer to how controls respond to issues, while control types refer to how they are implemented. Understanding both is critical.

3 Control Categories

CategoryAlso CalledWhat It CoversExamples
ManagerialAdministrative / "Soft" controlsAdminister the organization's assets and personnelPersonnel controls, data classification, data labeling, security awareness training, supervision
OperationalDay-to-day controlsPart of the organization's daily security stanceDirective controls, deterrent controls, security procedures
TechnicalLogical controlsSoftware/hardware components restricting accessFirewalls, IDS/IPS, encryption, authentication systems
Professor Duke: "When I do inspections, security awareness training is the one administrative control that's most overlooked. It blows me away. How do you keep your team abreast of the latest threats without training?"

6 Control Types

TypePurposeExamplesKey Note
PreventiveStop attacks before they happenLocks, badges, biometrics, encryption, IPS, antivirus, security guardsCan be physical, network, or operational security
DetectiveDetect an attack while occurringIDS, logs, guards, investigators, monitoringLike a doorbell — alerts you to activity
CorrectiveFix/restore after an attackFire extinguishers, isolating connections, new firewall rules, patching, deploying CERT teamReduces the effect of an attack
DeterrentDeter or discourage attackersUser ID/auth, fences, lighting, organizational security policies, NDAsOften trigger preventive or corrective controls. Attacks discovered early.
DirectiveSpecify acceptable practicesAUP (most popular), organizational security policies and proceduresEfficient ONLY if there is a stated CONSEQUENCE for not following directions
PhysicalProtect facilities and personnelPerimeter security, badges, swipe cards, guard dogs, man traps, biometrics, cablingPersonnel concerns should take priority over all other concerns
Slide 56 — "Important": Directive controls specify acceptable practices within the organization. The most popular directive control is an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP). Keep in mind that directive controls are efficient only if there is a stated consequence for not following the organization's directions. Without consequences, people shrug it off.
📋 7. Audits, Compliance & Continuous Monitoring
Critical Distinction — Don't Muddy the Waters! Compliance = THE LAW (non-negotiable, do it first)
Risk Management = Countermeasures, controls, safeguards (everything else)
Professor Duke: "Compliance is where we talk about the law. Risk management is where we talk about countermeasures, security controls, and safeguards. A printer going down is NOT a compliance issue — it's a risk management issue. If a safeguard isn't working, it doesn't mean you're non-compliant. It means the control has failed and needs to be mitigated."

SSAE-18 & SOC Reports

In 2011, SSAE (Statement on Standards for Attestation Engagements) No. 16 replaced SAS 70 as the authoritative standard for auditing service organizations, and was subsequently updated to version 18. These audits verify that controls and processes are actually followed.

ReportScopeWhat It Covers
SOC 1, Type 1Point-in-time snapshotAuditor's opinion of the accuracy and completeness of management's design of controls
SOC 1, Type 2Over a time periodIncludes everything in Type 1 PLUS an audit on the effectiveness of the controls over a certain time period

Both provide benchmarks for controls and are important tools for verifying security posture.

Continuous Monitoring

Before continuous monitoring can be successful, an organization must ensure that operational baselines are captured. You cannot recognize abnormal patterns of behavior if you don't know what normal looks like. Baselines should be revisited periodically to ensure they haven't changed.

SCT (Security Compliance Toolkit): An example of a continuous monitoring tool. It can monitor compliance with a baseline and works with two other Microsoft tools: Group Policy and Microsoft Endpoint Configuration Manager (MECM). These tools are GUI-based, user-friendly, and effective.

Account Management

Elements of proper account management:

  • Establish a formal process for establishing, issuing, and closing user accounts
  • Periodically review user accounts
  • Implement a process for tracking access authorization
  • Periodically rescreen personnel in sensitive positions (think Snowden)
  • Periodically verify the legitimacy of user accounts

Use a standardized naming convention (e.g., first.last). Review account management with your team once every three months.

Data Retention & Work Product

Data Retention: Security professional acts in advisory role only. Data owners/stakeholders determine the best retention policy. One-size-fits-all is impossible. Review periodically and train personnel.

Work Product Retention: Organizations need a clear policy defining all work product as property of the organization. Employees must sign an agreement — prevents them from leaving with proprietary work.
