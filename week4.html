SEAS8405 · Spring 2026 · Prof. Wayne Le · GWU
Architecture's Primary Purpose

The fundamental purpose of security architecture is to SUPPORT THE MISSION and enable business goals. Security is not an end in itself — it exists to protect and enable.

Enable mission by protecting what matters.

The WHY Before the HOW

Understand business drivers before technical implementation. A common mistake is jumping directly to technology selection without understanding the underlying need.

Example: MFA to prevent unauthorized access — ask WHY until you reach the mission. Why prevent unauthorized access? → To protect sensitive data. → Why protect sensitive data? → To enable customer trust. → Why enable customer trust? → To drive business profitability and compliance.

Business Enablement

Security measures are only useful when attached to mission (profitability, service delivery, compliance). "Our job is to make security indispensable by tying security back to business objectives."

Mission Alignment

Every architectural decision should trace back to organizational goals. "If it doesn't enable the mission, question whether it belongs in the architecture."

Understanding Organizational Goals — The Cascade

Organizational goals flow through multiple levels, each supporting the one above:

  • Enterprise Level: Why does the org exist? (Profitability, public service, patient care)
  • Technology Level: How does tech enable the enterprise mission? (Reliable infrastructure, digital services, data analytics)
  • Security Level: How does security protect and enable technology goals? (Prevent breaches, ensure compliance, protect IP)

This cascade connects specific security decisions to organizational purpose. Each level informs the constraints and requirements of the level below.

AAA Framework

Complete access management relies on three interconnected components:

Authentication

Proving identity — "Who are you?"

Verifying the user is who they claim to be.

Authorization

Granting permissions — "What can you do?"

Determining what an authenticated user is allowed to access.

Accounting: Tracking activity — "What did you do?" Recording user actions for audit and compliance.

These three work together for complete access management. You cannot have effective security with only one or two of these components.

Authentication Factors

Authentication can be based on multiple factor categories:

Factor Type Examples Strength
Something you KNOW Password, PIN, security questions Easy to use, easy to compromise
Something you HAVE Smart card, token, mobile phone Harder to compromise, requires possession
Something you ARE Fingerprint, face scan, retina scan (biometrics) Unique, cannot be forgotten or stolen
Somewhere you ARE Location-based, geofencing Can be spoofed, useful in combination
Something you DO Behavioral biometrics, typing patterns Continuous authentication, hard to replicate

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Critical: MFA combines 2+ different factor TYPES. Using password + security question = NOT MFA (both are "something you know"). They must be from different categories.

Example: Password (know) + authenticator app (have) = MFA. Password (know) + recovery codes (know) = NOT MFA.

Access Control Models
Model How It Works Use Case Pros & Cons
RBAC (Role-Based) Permissions based on job roles Most common in enterprise. "Nurse role" has access to patient records but not billing Simple to implement, scales well, but can create role explosion
ABAC (Attribute-Based) Permissions based on attributes (user, resource, environment) More flexible. "Allow access if user.department=Finance AND time=business_hours AND location=office" Very flexible, fine-grained control, but complex to manage
MAC (Mandatory) System-enforced, label-based. Users cannot change permissions Government/military. Clearance levels (Top Secret, Secret, etc) Very secure, cannot be overridden, but inflexible
DAC (Discretionary) Owner controls permissions File owner decides who can read/write Most flexible but least secure, prone to over-sharing

When to Use Each Model

  • RBAC: Default choice for most enterprises. Easy to understand and maintain.
  • ABAC: When you need fine-grained control based on context (time, location, data sensitivity).
  • MAC: High-security environments where users should not control access (government, defense).
  • DAC: Avoid in enterprise settings; used primarily in file systems and peer-to-peer scenarios.
Least Privilege Principle
Users should only have minimum access needed to perform their job. No more, no less.

Why It Matters

  • Reduces attack surface: A compromised account with limited permissions has limited damage potential
  • Compliance: Required by many regulatory frameworks (PCI DSS, HIPAA, SOX)
  • Audit trail clarity: Easier to identify anomalous activity when users shouldn't have certain permissions

The Challenge

Least privilege must be actively managed. As roles change and new responsibilities are added, access should be reviewed regularly. Over time, users accumulate permissions (privilege creep).

Just-In-Time (JIT) Access

Temporary elevated privileges only when needed, with automatic revocation after the task is complete. Example: DBAs requesting 1-hour admin access to patch production database, then automatic downgrade.

Federation and SSO

Federation

A trust relationship between organizations. Users authenticate once in their home organization and access resources across multiple federated organizations.

Single Sign-On (SSO)

One login, multiple applications within an organization. Reduces password fatigue, improves usability, simplifies account management.

Key Protocols

Protocol Purpose Use Case Format
SAML Enterprise SSO and federation Exchanges auth assertions between identity provider and service provider XML-based
OAuth 2.0 Authorization framework Delegates access without sharing credentials. "Sign in with Google/Facebook" JSON-based
OIDC Identity layer on OAuth 2.0 Adds authentication to OAuth's authorization. Returns ID token with user info JSON Web Tokens (JWT)
LDAP/Active Directory Centralized user management Stores user info, auth data, group memberships Directory protocol
Key distinction: OAuth 2.0 grants permission but doesn't prove identity. OIDC adds authentication on top, proving who you are and providing user information.
Privileged Access Management (PAM)

Managing and securing accounts with elevated privileges (admin accounts, service accounts, root).

These are the highest-value targets for attackers. Compromise of a privileged account = game over for the attacker (full access to systems and data).

Key PAM Controls

  • Password vaulting: Encrypt and centrally store admin credentials
  • Session recording: Audit all actions taken by privileged users
  • Just-in-time elevation: Grant admin access only when needed, auto-revoke after
  • Automatic rotation: Change passwords on a schedule without user knowledge
  • Multi-approval: Require multiple admins to approve sensitive actions
PAM is not optional in mature security architectures. It should be one of your highest implementation priorities.
Impossible Travel and Behavioral Detection

Impossible Travel Detection: A behavioral analytics technique that flags when a user authenticates from two geographically distant locations in a timeframe that makes physical travel impossible.

  • Example: User logs in from Washington DC at 2:00 PM, then from Moscow at 2:15 PM. No flight covers that distance in 15 minutes — this is an impossible travel event.
  • How it works: SIEM or identity platform calculates distance between login locations and compares against time elapsed. If speed required exceeds ~600 mph, flag it.
  • Response: Lock account, require step-up authentication, alert SOC for investigation
  • Limitations: VPN usage can create false positives. Shared accounts complicate detection. Need to account for legitimate proxy/VPN patterns.
KEY: Impossible travel is a prime example of context-aware authentication — using behavioral signals beyond just credentials to detect compromise.
Scattered Spider Attack Case Study

Scattered Spider (UNC3944): A threat group that targeted major organizations (MGM Resorts, Caesars Entertainment, 2023) using social engineering against identity systems.

  • Attack method: Called IT help desks impersonating employees, convinced agents to reset MFA tokens, then used those tokens to gain access
  • Why it matters for IAM: Even strong MFA can be defeated if the reset/recovery process is weak. The help desk became the weakest link.
  • Lessons for architects:
    • Help desk identity verification procedures are a critical IAM control
    • MFA reset workflows need the same rigor as initial enrollment
    • Behavioral analytics (impossible travel, unusual device) could have caught the post-compromise activity
    • PAM controls on admin accounts would have limited lateral movement
Prof. Le's Point: "Scattered Spider proves that identity is the new perimeter. Your IAM architecture is only as strong as its weakest recovery pathway."
Risk Appetite Drives Design

Risk appetite: The amount and type of risk an organization is willing to accept to achieve its business objectives.

Risk appetite varies significantly by industry, organization size, maturity, and regulatory environment:

Sector Risk Appetite Rationale
Financial Services Very Low Regulatory penalties are extremely costly; customer trust is paramount
Healthcare Very Low (patient safety), Moderate (admin) Patient safety cannot be compromised; administrative losses are more acceptable
SaaS Startups Higher Speed to market is critical; some security debt may be acceptable initially
Government/Military Extremely Low National security and classified information at stake
Architecture must match YOUR organization's risk appetite, not generic best practices. A highly regulated healthcare system and a startup have fundamentally different security architectures.
Alignment: Fit with Organization

A "perfect" security architecture that doesn't fit your organization is worse than a simpler one that works. Consider these dimensions:

Culture Fit

Startup culture values speed and agility; enterprise culture values stability and compliance. A security architecture must match your culture or it will be circumvented.

Skills Match

Don't choose forensic-grade incident response tools if you lack forensic analysts on staff. Your architecture should match your team's capabilities or include plans to hire/train.

Change Tolerance

Healthcare systems operating 24/7 have zero tolerance for maintenance windows. A software company might accept planned downtime. Architecture must respect operational realities.

Risk Appetite

Some organizations accept more risk for agility and speed; others minimize risk at the cost of operational friction. Be intentional about your risk tolerance.

Core principle: Sustainability over perfection. An architecture your team can actually operate and maintain beats a theoretically "better" architecture that creates unsustainable burden.
Efficiency: Opportunity Cost

Every choice has a cost. Opportunity cost = what you give up by choosing one option over another.

Example Scenario

Budget: $500,000 + 3 senior engineers for 12 months

Option A: Forensic-grade SIEM with 24/7 SOC

  • Cost: $500K (software, infrastructure, licensing)
  • Team: All 3 engineers
  • Outcome: Excellent detection and response

Option B: Basic EDR + cloud logging

  • Cost: $200K (software, infrastructure)
  • Team: 1 engineer for operations
  • Freed resources: $300K budget + 2 engineers for other priorities

Choosing Option A means you cannot do Option B. The opportunity cost of Option A is the $300K + 2 engineer-years you could have invested in vulnerability management, architecture hardening, or security training.

Ask not just what the solution costs, but what else we could do with these resources.
