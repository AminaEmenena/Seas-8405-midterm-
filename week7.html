Week 7 — Architectural Decision Making

SEAS8405 · Spring 2026 · Prof. Wayne Le · GWU
1. What is Architectural Decision Making?

Systematic process of evaluating options, weighing trade-offs, selecting approaches to best serve organizational goals within real-world constraints.

KEY IDEA: Architectural decisions are rarely about finding the "perfect" solution—they're about making defensible trade-offs that serve organizational goals within real-world constraints.

Constant trade-offs. It's hard — judgment, communication, solving short-term problems while thinking long-term, managing organizational culture.

  • Trade-Off Analysis: every decision involves trade-offs between security, cost, performance, usability, complexity, time to implement.
  • Stakeholder Alignment: must balance competing needs — security teams want defense in depth, executives want cost efficiency, users want convenience.
  • Defensibility: must document rationale so decisions can be defended during audits, after incidents, when questioned. "If we can't explain decisions, we are vulnerable."
  • Future Adaptability: good decisions account for future growth, changing threats, technology evolution.
Remember This
"No Perfect Solutions, Only Defensible Trade-offs"
2. Common Decision-Making Challenges
KEY: The biggest decision-making trap is premature convergence—jumping to a solution before fully exploring the problem and alternatives.
  • Incomplete Information: must decide before all facts known. Requirements unclear, threats evolving, future direction uncertain. Architects rarely have perfect information.
  • Conflicting Requirements: security conflicts with usability, performance, cost. Business wants innovation and speed; security needs control. Finding balance is constant challenge. "Continuous negotiation and reevaluation."
  • Legacy Constraints: existing technical debt, sunk costs. Must architect AROUND constraints you cannot remove. "Creativity — how to solve threats under constraint."
  • Resource Limitations: finite budget, limited personnel, skills gaps, aggressive timelines. "Perfect solution exists in theory, not affordable or achievable. Must optimize within constraints."
Prof. Le's Tip: "The smartest architects I've worked with don't have all the answers—they ask the right questions. They force the team to think through WHY constraints exist and whether they're truly immovable."
3. Decision-Making Framework (5 Steps)
KEY IDEA: The 5-step framework is not rigid—it's a checkpoint system to ensure you're thinking holistically before committing to a decision.
  • Step 1: Define the Problem — clearly articulate WHAT decision needs to be made and WHY it matters. What are success criteria? Be specific.
  • Step 2: Identify Options — generate multiple potential solutions. Avoid premature convergence. Include status quo as option to evaluate.
  • Step 3: Evaluate Trade-offs — assess each option against multiple criteria: security effectiveness, cost, complexity, timeline, organizational fit, scalability.
  • Step 4: Make Decision and Document — select best option. Document decision rationale, alternatives considered, trade-offs accepted, assumptions made.
  • Step 5: Monitor and Adapt — after implementation, monitor outcomes. Be willing to adapt if circumstances change or assumptions prove wrong.
Remember This
"Define → Identify → Evaluate → Document → Monitor"
4. Evaluation Criteria for Architectural Decisions

Security and Risk Criteria:

  • Effectiveness against threats
  • Residual risk after implementation
  • Defense in depth and redundancy
  • Resilience against failures
  • Compliance and regulatory alignment

Business and Operational Criteria:

  • Total cost of ownership over lifecycle
  • Implementation timeline and complexity
  • Operational overhead and maintenance burden
  • Scalability and future adaptability
  • User experience and productivity impact
  • Skills required and team capacity
KEY IDEA: Good architectural decisions balance BOTH security effectiveness AND business viability. Choosing maximum security at any cost OR minimum cost regardless of risk both fail.
Best Practice: Use weighted scoring matrix to evaluate options. Assign weights to each criterion based on organizational priorities, then score each option systematically. Transparency prevents politics from dominating the decision.
5. Build vs Buy vs Managed Service

Build Custom:

  • Pros — perfect fit, full control, no vendor lock-in.
  • Cons — high development cost, long timeline, ongoing maintenance, skills retention risk.

Buy Commercial:

  • Pros — faster deployment, proven technology, vendor support.
  • Cons — licensing costs, may not fit perfectly, vendor dependency, integration complexity, product discontinuation risk.

Managed Service:

  • Pros — expertise without hiring, 24/7 coverage, scalable, predictable cost.
  • Cons — less control, data sharing concerns, vendor dependency, may not understand your environment.
KEY: There is no universally "best" option. The right choice depends entirely on organizational maturity, available resources, team skills, and whether the capability is strategic or commodity.

When to Build: highly unique requirements, skilled internal team with capacity, long-term strategic capability, acceptable to take time. Example: custom threat intelligence platform.

When to Buy/Manage: commodity capability, talent shortage, need rapid deployment, want team focused on strategic work. Example: EDR, SIEM, SOC operations.

Prof. Le's Tip: "Most organizations over-build and under-buy. We love building things because it feels like we're in control. But that control costs money and focus. Ask yourself: is this a competitive advantage, or is it table stakes? If it's table stakes, buy it."
Remember This
"Strategic = Build, Commodity = Buy or Manage"
6. Decision-Making Checklist (8 Items)
Best Practice: Use this checklist before finalizing ANY architectural decision. These 8 items prevent costly mistakes and ensure you're thinking comprehensively.
  1. Have I clearly defined the problem and success criteria?
  2. Have I identified at least 3 options including status quo?
  3. Have I evaluated trade-offs across security, cost, time, complexity, and organizational fit?
  4. Have I considered stakeholder perspectives: security, business, users, operations?
  5. Have I documented decision rationale and alternatives considered?
  6. Have I identified key assumptions and dependencies?
  7. Have I defined metrics to evaluate whether decision is working?
  8. Have I planned for monitoring and adaptation if circumstances change?
KEY IDEA: If you can't answer "yes" to all 8 items, you're not ready to decide. The checklist forces alignment and prevents surprises later.
7. Key Takeaways from Week 7
KEY IDEA: Architecture is not an individual sport. Success depends on understanding organizational context, building stakeholder alignment, and being willing to defend—and change—decisions based on outcomes.
  • Every Decision is Trade-Off: no perfect solutions. Good architects make trade-offs explicit and defensible.
  • Context is Critical: right decision depends on resources, maturity, constraints, culture. What works elsewhere may not work for you.
  • Measured Outcomes: define success metrics upfront. Track whether achieving results. Adapt if not working.
  • Collaborate Don't Dictate: architecture succeeds through partnership — with stakeholders, vendors, business units. "Ivory tower mandates fail."
  • Learn from Others: study real-world cases, understand WHY decisions were made, adapt lessons to your context.
Prof. Le's Tip: "The best architects I've worked with are humble. They know they'll be wrong sometimes, so they over-communicate their decisions and actively seek feedback. That's how you catch mistakes early."
